[(c)] Newton's Law of Cooling gives the temperature of an object at time \(t\) as \(f(t)=[f(0)-A]e^{kt}+A\text{,}\) where \(A\) is the ambient temperature surrounding the object. In our case, the ambient temperature is 0 degrees. In an object whose temperature is being modelled by Newton's Law of Cooling, it doesn't matter whether the object is heating or cooling, \(k\) is negative. We saw this in Question 3.3.4.3 of Section 3.3.2, but it bears repeating. Since \(f(t)\) approaches the ambient temperature (in this case, 0) as \(t\) goes to infinty:
\begin{equation*} \lim_{t \to \infty}100e^{kt}=0 \end{equation*}
so \(k\) is negative.
